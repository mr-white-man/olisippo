You've built the game. The build is stable. You're ready to ship.

You are not ready to ship.

Here's everything I wish someone had told me before we launched Void Protocol.

## The Launch Window is Not the Launch

Steam's new release algorithm front-loads visibility in the first 2 weeks. Every bug that slips through, every missing feature in your store page, every delay in responding to reviews — these compound asymmetrically during that window.

We spent 3 weeks before launch doing nothing but building the store page, trailer, press kit, and Discord server. That was the right call. We also spent 3 weeks fixing bugs we found during that process. That was also the right call.

Ship slow so launch can be fast.

## Legal Exists

- Get a business entity before you collect money. Sole proprietorship minimum, LLC preferred.
- Your music licensing needs to cover streaming platforms *and* content creation. These are different licenses. Check both.
- GDPR applies to you if any EU player downloads your game. Analytics, crash reporters, even Steam's own regional data — you need a privacy policy.
- Platform fees, payment processor fees, and distributor cuts all compound. Know your effective margin before you set a price.

## The Reviews You Don't Respond To

Negative reviews will arrive. Some will be fair. Some will be unhinged. The correct response to both is: don't respond publicly when you're angry.

I responded to a negative review at 2am, 3 days post-launch. We'd shipped a major bug in a patch. I was defensive. It read poorly. That exchange lives forever on the store page.

Respond to reviews when you've slept. Respond professionally. Respond with patches, not arguments.

## You Will Want to Quit

Three to four weeks post-launch, after the initial visibility bump fades and sales plateau, you will enter a psychological valley. The energy that sustained crunch is gone. The dopamine of launch-week numbers is gone. The game is out and the world is... fine with it.

This is normal. It's not failure.

Build something small. Write. Take a weekend off. Then come back and start the post-mortem. The lessons in that valley are the ones that make your next game better.
